The Low FODMAP diet is one of the most powerful dietary interventions for managing irritable bowel syndrome (IBS), small intestinal bacterial overgrowth (SIBO), and other functional digestive disorders. Developed by researchers at Monash University in Melbourne, Australia, the diet has been validated in over 30 clinical trials and is now recommended as a first-line treatment for IBS by the American College of Gastroenterology.

Despite its clinical pedigree, the Low FODMAP diet is widely misunderstood. It is not a "diet" in the traditional sense — you do not follow it forever. It is a diagnostic elimination protocol designed to identify your personal trigger foods. When done correctly, it takes 8 to 12 weeks from start to finish and results in a customized, sustainable diet that you can follow long-term without feeling deprived.

This guide walks you through every phase of the Low FODMAP diet, from strict elimination to systematic reintroduction, with food lists, meal plans, and expert tips to help you succeed.

What Are FODMAPs?

FODMAP is an acronym that stands for Fermentable Oligosaccharides, Disaccharides, Monosaccharides, and Polyols. These are short-chain carbohydrates that are poorly absorbed in the small intestine. Instead of being absorbed, they travel to the large intestine, where they are rapidly fermented by gut bacteria, producing gas and drawing water into the bowel.

There are five categories of FODMAPs:

  • Oligosaccharides (fructans and GOS): Found in wheat, rye, onions, garlic, legumes, and certain nuts. These are chains of fructose or galactose molecules linked together. Humans lack the enzyme to break the bonds, so they reach the colon undigested.
  • Disaccharides (lactose): Found in milk, yogurt, soft cheese, and ice cream. Lactose requires the enzyme lactase for digestion. Many adults naturally lose lactase production after childhood, leading to lactose malabsorption.
  • Monosaccharides (excess fructose): Found in apples, pears, mangoes, honey, and high-fructose corn syrup. When fructose exceeds glucose in a food, the excess fructose is poorly absorbed.
  • Polyols (sugar alcohols): Found in stone fruits (cherries, plums, peaches), mushrooms, and artificial sweeteners ending in -ol (sorbitol, mannitol, xylitol, maltitol). These are sugar alcohols that are passively absorbed — slowly and incompletely.

When these carbohydrates reach the large intestine, bacteria ferment them rapidly, producing hydrogen, methane, or hydrogen sulfide gas. This causes distension, bloating, pain, and altered motility. For individuals with visceral hypersensitivity (a heightened awareness of intestinal sensations common in IBS), even normal amounts of gas can cause severe discomfort.

The genius of the Low FODMAP diet is not that it eliminates all FODMAPs forever. It is that it systematically removes the variables, so you can identify exactly which types and what amounts of FODMAPs trigger your symptoms. Some people react to fructans but not lactose. Others handle sorbitol but not excess fructose. The diet gives you precise data about your body.

Who Should Try the Low FODMAP Diet?

The Low FODMAP diet is not for everyone. It is specifically designed for individuals with diagnosed or strongly suspected functional gastrointestinal disorders. You are a good candidate if you experience:

  • Chronic bloating and distension that improves with fasting
  • Abdominal pain or cramping associated with bowel movements
  • Diarrhea, constipation, or alternating bowel habits (IBS pattern)
  • Excessive gas and flatulence
  • Nausea after meals
  • A diagnosis of IBS, SIBO, or functional dyspepsia

If you have a history of disordered eating, are underweight, or are pregnant or breastfeeding, the Low FODMAP diet should only be attempted under the supervision of a registered dietitian. The elimination phase is restrictive, and it is essential to ensure you are meeting your nutritional needs.

Phase 1: Elimination (Days 1–30)

The elimination phase is the foundation of the entire protocol. For 4 to 6 weeks, you will eat only foods that are low in FODMAPs. This gives your gut a chance to "reset" — the bacterial populations that feed on FODMAPs will die back, and your symptoms should resolve if FODMAPs are a significant trigger for you.

Goals of the Elimination Phase

  1. Symptom resolution — achieve a baseline of minimal to no digestive symptoms.
  2. Identify whether FODMAPs are a primary trigger for your symptoms.
  3. Build a foundation for the reintroduction phase.

Rules of the Elimination Phase

  • Eat only foods from the "low FODMAP" list for every meal and snack.
  • No "cheat meals" — even a single high-FODMAP meal can trigger symptoms that take days to resolve and corrupt your baseline data.
  • Keep a symptom journal. Record what you eat, when you eat, and any symptoms that arise (type, severity, timing).
  • Stay hydrated. Many low FODMAP foods are lower in fiber, which can cause constipation. Drink at least 2 liters of water per day.
  • Consider a multivitamin. The elimination phase eliminates many nutrient-dense foods, including most fruits, legumes, and whole grains. A basic multivitamin with minerals fills the gaps.

Most people notice improvement within the first 7 to 10 days. Bloating typically resolves first, followed by improvements in bowel regularity. By week 3, if FODMAPs are a primary trigger, you should be experiencing significantly fewer symptoms. If you have not noticed any improvement after 4 weeks, FODMAPs may not be your primary trigger. Move on to the reintroduction phase anyway — some people only discover their triggers during reintroduction, when symptoms reappear.

Complete Food Lists: What to Eat and Avoid

Below are comprehensive lists organized by food group. These lists are based on Monash University's latest testing data (updated 2026).

Vegetables: Low FODMAP (Eat Freely)

Bell peppers (all colors), bok choy, broccoli (heads only, 3/4 cup limit), Brussels sprouts (2 sprouts limit), cabbage (common), carrots, celery (1 stalk limit), chives, cucumber, eggplant, green beans (15 bean limit), kale, lettuce (all types), olives, parsnips, potatoes, pumpkin (1/3 cup limit), radishes, spinach (5 cups limit), spring onion (green part only), squash (butternut 1/4 cup limit), swiss chard, tomatoes (1 medium limit), turnips, zucchini.

Vegetables: High FODMAP (Avoid)

Asparagus, artichokes, beets, broccoli (large amounts), Brussels sprouts (large amounts), cabbage (savoy), cauliflower, celery root, fennel, garlic, leeks, mushrooms (all), okra, onions (all), peas (snow, snap), shallots, sweet corn.

Fruits: Low FODMAP (Eat Freely)

Bananas (firm, unripe), blueberries (1/3 cup limit), cantaloupe, cranberries, dragon fruit, grapes, kiwifruit (2 limit), lemons, limes, mandarins, oranges, papaya, passion fruit, pineapple, plantain, raspberries (2/3 cup limit), rhubarb (1/2 cup limit), strawberries (5 medium limit).

Fruits: High FODMAP (Avoid)

Apples, apricots, avocados (more than 1/4), blackberries, cherries, dates, figs, grapefruit, mangoes, nectarines, peaches, pears, persimmons, plums, prunes, raisins, watermelon.

Proteins: Low FODMAP (Eat Freely)

Beef, chicken (plain), eggs, fish, lamb, pork, shellfish, tofu (firm, plain), turkey. All plain, unprocessed meats and fish are naturally FODMAP-free.

Proteins: High FODMAP (Avoid)

Processed meats with added garlic or onion powder, sausages, most deli meats, marinated meats (check ingredients), legumes (chickpeas, lentils, kidney beans, black beans), baked beans, soy milk (from whole soybeans).

Grains: Low FODMAP (Eat in Moderation)

Oats (1/2 cup dry), white rice, brown rice, rice noodles, quinoa (1/2 cup cooked), sourdough spelt bread (2 slices), corn tortillas, gluten-free bread (check for no high-FODMAP additives), cornflakes, puffed rice.

Grains: High FODMAP (Avoid)

Wheat-based bread, pasta, and baked goods; rye bread; barley; couscous; semolina; most breakfast cereals (check labels); legumes and pulses.

Dairy: Low FODMAP (Eat in Moderation)

Lactose-free milk, lactose-free yogurt, hard cheeses (cheddar, parmesan, Swiss, colby), brie (25g limit), camembert (25g limit), feta (50g limit), butter, ghee.

Dairy: High FODMAP (Avoid)

Cow's milk (regular), goat's milk, sheep's milk, yogurt (regular), soft fresh cheeses (ricotta, cottage, cream cheese), ice cream, custard, buttermilk.

Nuts and Seeds: Low FODMAP

Almonds (10 nuts), Brazil nuts, chia seeds, macadamias, peanuts (32 nuts), pecans (10 halves), pine nuts, pumpkin seeds, sesame seeds, sunflower seeds, walnuts (5 halves).

Nuts and Seeds: High FODMAP (Avoid)

Cashews, pistachios, nut butters with high-FODMAP additives (check labels), large quantities of any nut.

7-Day Meal Plan and Prep Guide

Consistency is the key to success on the elimination phase. Meal prepping on Sunday for the week ahead dramatically reduces the mental load and prevents slip-ups. Here is a complete 7-day meal plan with a Sunday prep guide.

Sunday Meal Prep (1 Hour)

  • Cook 2 cups of white rice (makes ~6 cups cooked).
  • Grill 6 chicken breasts seasoned with salt, pepper, and chives.
  • Wash and chop: bell peppers, carrots, zucchini, spinach, kale.
  • Make a low FODMAP vinaigrette: 3 parts olive oil, 1 part red wine vinegar, salt, pepper, fresh oregano.
  • Hard-boil 6 eggs.
  • Portion lactose-free yogurt into single-serving containers with strawberries and blueberries.
  • Make a large batch of low FODMAP vegetable soup (carrots, zucchini, spinach in chicken bone broth).

Day 1: Monday

Breakfast: 2 scrambled eggs with spinach and tomatoes. 1 cup of lactose-free milk.

Lunch: Grilled chicken breast over mixed greens with bell peppers, carrots, and low FODMAP vinaigrette.

Snack: 10 almonds + 5 strawberries.

Dinner: Grilled salmon with roasted zucchini and bell peppers, side of white rice.

Day 2: Tuesday

Breakfast: Oatmeal (1/2 cup dry oats) made with water, topped with blueberries and 1 tbsp peanut butter.

Lunch: Leftover salmon and roasted vegetables.

Snack: Hard-boiled egg + small orange.

Dinner: Chicken stir-fry with bok choy, carrots, and rice noodles with tamari (gluten-free soy sauce) and ginger.

Day 3: Wednesday

Breakfast: Lactose-free yogurt (1 cup) with 1/3 cup blueberries and 2 tbsp pumpkin seeds.

Lunch: Tuna salad (canned tuna, olive oil, chives, chopped cucumber) over spinach.

Snack: Rice cake with peanut butter.

Dinner: Beef and bell pepper skewers (grilled) with quinoa (1/2 cup cooked) and mixed greens.

Day 4: Thursday

Breakfast: 2-egg omelet with chives and shredded cheddar, side of spinach.

Lunch: Leftover beef skewers with quinoa and greens.

Snack: 1 firm banana + 1 tbsp almond butter.

Dinner: Baked cod with roasted potatoes (with olive oil and rosemary) and steamed green beans.

Day 5: Friday

Breakfast: Smoothie: 1 cup spinach, 1/3 cup blueberries, 1 cup lactose-free milk, 1 scoop rice protein powder.

Lunch: Leftover cod with roasted potatoes and green beans.

Snack: 5 strawberries + 10 macadamia nuts.

Dinner: Grilled pork chops with roasted butternut squash (1/3 cup) and a side salad.

Day 6: Saturday

Breakfast: 2 poached eggs over a bed of sauteed spinach and tomatoes.

Lunch: Chicken and rice soup (homemade with bone broth, chicken, carrots, celery, chives).

Snack: 1 small orange + handful of walnuts (5 halves).

Dinner: Shrimp and vegetable skewers (bell peppers, zucchini, cherry tomatoes) with rice.

Day 7: Sunday

Breakfast: Gluten-free toast (2 slices) with peanut butter and a small banana.

Lunch: Leftover shrimp and vegetable skewers with rice.

Snack: 1 cup lactose-free yogurt with 1/3 cup raspberries.

Dinner: Roasted chicken thighs with carrots and parsnips, side of spinach salad.

Phase 2: Reintroduction (Weeks 5–8)

After 4 to 6 weeks of elimination, transition to the reintroduction phase. This is arguably the most important phase — it is where you identify exactly which FODMAP categories trigger your symptoms and at what doses.

How Reintroduction Works

You systematically challenge one FODMAP category at a time using a standardized food challenge. Each challenge takes 3 days, and you test only one category per week. Here is the protocol:

Day 1: Eat a small amount of a pure FODMAP source (e.g., 1/2 an apple for excess fructose). Eat it on an empty stomach and do not consume any other high-FODMAP foods that day. Record any symptoms over the next 4 hours and again the next morning.

Day 2: Eat a larger amount of the same FODMAP (e.g., 1 whole apple). Record symptoms.

Day 3: Eat a very large amount (e.g., 2 apples). Record symptoms.

Days 4–7: Return to strict low FODMAP eating to allow any symptoms to resolve completely before testing the next category.

Recommended Challenge Order

  1. Lactose: Challenge with 1 cup, 1.5 cups, and 2 cups of lactose-free milk (not to worry — you use regular milk for this test). Using milk as the test vehicle isolates lactose.
  2. Fructose (excess): Challenge with 1/2 apple, 1 apple, 2 apples. Apples are a pure source of excess fructose.
  3. Fructans: Challenge with 1/4 tsp garlic-infused oil + 1 slice wheat bread, then increase to 1 tsp garlic oil + 2 slices wheat bread, then 2 tsp garlic oil + 3 slices wheat bread.
  4. Polyols (sorbitol): Challenge with 1/2, 1, and 2 dried apricots or sugar-free gum sweetened with sorbitol.
  5. Polyols (mannitol): Challenge with 1/4 cup, 1/2 cup, and 1 cup of mushrooms (canned, well-rinsed).
  6. GOS (galacto-oligosaccharides): Challenge with 1/4 cup, 1/2 cup, and 3/4 cup of chickpeas (canned, well-rinsed).

Record your tolerance level for each category. You may discover that you tolerate small amounts of a FODMAP but react to large amounts — this is normal and useful information for designing your long-term diet.

Phase 3: Modified Long-Term Diet

This is your permanent way of eating. Based on your reintroduction data, you now know exactly which FODMAPs you tolerate and in what quantities. The modified diet is not "low FODMAP" — it is "your FODMAP diet." You may tolerate all FODMAPs except fructans, in which case your diet looks similar to a regular diet but with onion and garlic avoided. Or you may tolerate all FODMAPs in moderate amounts, meaning you can eat a completely normal diet with occasional adjustments.

Some general principles for the modified phase:

  • Eat as broadly as you comfortably can. Dietary diversity is the single strongest predictor of microbial diversity, and microbial diversity is the single strongest predictor of long-term gut health.
  • Use the "stacking" concept. You may tolerate a small apple alone, but an apple plus grapes plus a slice of wheat bread may trigger symptoms because the total FODMAP load exceeds your threshold. Stacking is the most commonly overlooked factor in long-term FODMAP management.
  • Re-test periodically. Many people find that their tolerance improves over time as their gut heals. What triggers symptoms today may be well-tolerated in 6 to 12 months.

5 Common Mistakes and How to Avoid Them

1. Not Reading Labels

Onion and garlic powder are everywhere — in salad dressings, spice blends, soup bases, sauces, and processed meats. You cannot rely on your memory; you must read every label. Better yet, make your own dressings and sauces during the elimination phase.

2. Eating Too Much of a Low FODMAP Food

Low FODMAP does not mean "zero FODMAP." Many foods on the "safe" list have a serving size limit — eating three servings of a low FODMAP food at once may push you over your threshold. Avocado is low FODMAP at 1/8 of an avocado but high FODMAP at 1/4 or more. Pay attention to serving sizes.

3. Forgetting About Stacking

Even if every food you eat in a meal is individually low FODMAP, the combined total may trigger symptoms. If you eat a meal with wheat-free bread (allowed), half a banana (allowed), and peanut butter (allowed), you may feel fine. Add a glass of orange juice (allowed) and a handful of almonds (allowed), and you might exceed your osmotically active carbohydrate threshold. This is stacking.

4. Not Working with a Dietitian

The Low FODMAP diet has a steep learning curve. Studies consistently show that patients who work with a registered dietitian experience better symptom relief and are more likely to complete the protocol successfully than those who attempt it alone. If you can afford it, invest in three to four sessions with an IBS-specialized dietitian.

5. Staying in the Elimination Phase Too Long

The elimination phase is nutritionally restrictive and can lead to deficiencies in calcium, vitamin D, fiber, and prebiotics if followed for more than 8 weeks. A 2018 study in Nutrients found that patients who remained on the elimination phase for more than 8 weeks had significantly lower microbial diversity and reduced butyrate-producing bacteria. Move into reintroduction after 4 to 6 weeks — do not delay.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is the Low FODMAP diet safe long-term?

The strict elimination phase is not intended for long-term use. However, the modified phase — your personal diet after reintroduction — is safe and can be followed indefinitely. It is essentially a whole foods diet that naturally avoids your specific trigger foods.

Can I drink alcohol on the Low FODMAP diet?

During the elimination phase, avoid most alcoholic beverages. High-FODMAP alcohols include: rum (high in sorbitol), sweet wines, beer (contains fructans), and mixed drinks with fruit juice or high-FODMAP sweeteners. Low-FODMAP options include: vodka, gin, whiskey, dry wines (red and white), and sake — in moderation (1 standard drink).

Is the Low FODMAP diet the same as gluten-free?

No. The Low FODMAP diet restricts fructans, which are found in wheat, rye, and barley — the same grains that contain gluten. However, the Low FODMAP diet also restricts many foods that are naturally gluten-free (onions, garlic, legumes, many fruits). The two diets overlap but are not the same.

Do I need the Monash University app?

Yes. The Monash University Low FODMAP Diet app is the gold standard resource for the diet. It is updated regularly with new food testing data and includes serving size guidance, a food diary, and a symptom tracker. At $9.99, it is the best investment you can make for the success of your diet.

Can children follow the Low FODMAP diet?

Children with IBS like symptoms can benefit from the Low FODMAP diet, but it must be done under the supervision of a pediatric dietitian to ensure adequate nutrition for growth and development. The elimination phase should be shorter (2 to 3 weeks) and the reintroduction phase more carefully structured to avoid nutritional gaps.

Will the Low FODMAP diet cure my IBS?

The Low FODMAP diet does not "cure" IBS, but it provides a framework for managing symptoms by identifying and avoiding trigger foods. Most patients who complete the protocol correctly report a 50 to 80% reduction in symptom severity. For many, this is the difference between daily suffering and a normal quality of life.
